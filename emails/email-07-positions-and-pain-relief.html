Now that you know the stages of labour, let's talk about two things that make a real difference: how you move and how you manage pain.

Your body is incredibly smart. If you let it move, it will naturally find positions that help your baby descend and rotate. And when it comes to pain relief, you have more options than you might think.

Positions that help

Upright & walking

Gravity is your friend. Walking, standing, and swaying during early and active labour helps baby move down. Lean against a wall or your partner during contractions.

Hands and knees (all fours)

Brilliant for back labour. Takes pressure off your spine, helps baby rotate into the best position, and gives you a sense of control. Rock your hips gently or make circles.

Birth ball

Sitting and gently bouncing or circling your hips on a birth ball opens your pelvis and keeps you upright. You can also lean over the ball while kneeling. Many hospitals have these available — ask for one.

Squatting

Opens your pelvis to its widest. Great for the pushing stage. Use your partner or a squat bar for support. Even a supported deep squat between contractions can help.

Side-lying

When you need to rest between contractions, lying on your side with a pillow between your knees keeps your pelvis open while letting you conserve energy. Much better than lying flat on your back.

Pain relief options

Natural methods

Breathing: Slow, deep breathing during contractions. In through your nose, out through your mouth. This is more powerful than it sounds.
Water: A warm shower on your back or labouring in a birth pool. Water is sometimes called "nature's epidural."
Massage & counter-pressure: Firm pressure on your lower back during contractions. Your partner can learn this — we'll practise on Saturday.
Heat packs: A warm wheat bag or hot water bottle on your lower back or belly.
Movement & vocalisation: Swaying, rocking, and making low sounds (moaning, humming) helps release tension.

Medical options

Epidural: An injection into your lower back that numbs the lower half of your body. Very effective for pain. You'll stay awake and alert but won't be able to walk. Available in most private hospitals.
Entonox (gas and air): You breathe it through a mouthpiece during contractions. It takes the edge off and wears off quickly. Available in some SA hospitals.
Pethidine: An injection that helps you relax between contractions. Can make you drowsy. Usually given in early-to-active labour, not close to delivery.

No judgment here

There's no medal for doing it without pain relief, and there's no failure in choosing it. The best approach is the one that helps you feel safe, supported, and present for your birth. Know your options, then decide in the moment.
