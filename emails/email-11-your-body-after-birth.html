Everyone focuses on pregnancy and birth, but very few people prepare you for what happens after. Your body has done something extraordinary, and it needs time to recover. Let's talk about what to expect and how to look after yourself.

Your body after a vaginal birth

Lochia (bleeding): You'll bleed for 2–6 weeks. It starts heavy and red, then gradually lightens. Stock up on maternity pads — not tampons.
Afterpains: Cramping as your uterus contracts back to its pre-pregnancy size. Stronger with subsequent babies. Paracetamol helps.
Perineal soreness: Whether you tore or had an episiotomy, it will be tender. Ice packs, sitz baths, and keeping clean with a peri bottle help.
Going to the loo: Your first wee might sting. Your first bowel movement might be daunting. Drink lots of water, eat fibre, and don't hold it in.

Your body after a C-section

Remember: You've had major abdominal surgery. Be gentle with yourself.
Movement: Getting up and walking gently helps recovery, but don't push it. No lifting anything heavier than your baby for 6 weeks.
Your scar: Keep it clean and dry. It may feel numb, itchy, or tender. These are all normal. Watch for signs of infection (redness, swelling, discharge).
Feeding positions: The rugby ball hold or side-lying position keeps baby off your incision.

The emotional side

Baby blues: Around day 3–5, hormones crash and many moms feel tearful, overwhelmed, or anxious. This is very common and usually passes within 1–2 weeks.
Postnatal depression: If sadness, anxiety, or inability to cope persists beyond 2 weeks, or you have frightening thoughts, please reach out to your midwife or doctor. This is not weakness — it's a medical condition that responds well to treatment.
Bonding: Not every mom feels an instant rush of love. For some, bonding builds gradually. Both are normal. Keep holding your baby, keep showing up — the bond will come.

Early parenting survival tips

Sleep when baby sleeps. The housework can wait. You cannot pour from an empty cup.
Accept help. When someone offers to bring food, do laundry, or hold the baby while you shower — say yes.
Lower your standards. The house will be messy. You will live in pyjamas. Your only job right now is to recover and care for your baby.
Limit visitors. You don't owe anyone access to your baby. "We're not ready for visitors yet" is a complete sentence.
Eat and drink. Keep water and snacks within arm's reach of wherever you feed. Breastfeeding makes you ravenously hungry and thirsty.
Take it one day at a time. Some days will be beautiful. Some will be hard. Both are normal. You are doing better than you think.
