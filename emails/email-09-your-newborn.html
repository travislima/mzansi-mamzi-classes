Welcome to our final week. You've learned about your pregnancy, prepared for birth — and now it's time to talk about the person you're doing all of this for: your baby.

Newborns are incredible. They're also a little surprising if you don't know what to expect. So let's talk about what's normal and what your baby needs from you.

What they actually look like

Instagram newborns and real newborns are not the same thing. Your baby might have:

•  A slightly cone-shaped head (from the birth canal — it rounds out quickly)
•  Puffy eyes and a squished face
•  White, waxy coating (vernix) — this protected their skin in the womb
•  Fine downy hair (lanugo), especially on shoulders and back
•  Blotchy, peeling skin — completely normal in the first week
•  Tiny hands that grip your finger with surprising strength

All of this is normal and beautiful in its own way. They've just done something extraordinary.

The golden hour

The first hour after birth is called the "golden hour" for a reason. Skin-to-skin contact in this time:

•  Regulates baby's temperature, heart rate, and breathing
•  Helps them colonise with your good bacteria
•  Triggers breastfeeding instincts — they may crawl towards the breast
•  Releases oxytocin for both of you (bonding and wellbeing)

Ask for uninterrupted skin-to-skin — weighing and measuring can wait. This is true for C-section births too.

How they communicate

Babies can't talk, but they tell you everything you need to know. Here are the early hunger cues to watch for (before crying):

Early cues: Stirring, mouth opening, turning head side to side, lip smacking
Mid cues: Stretching, increasing movement, hand to mouth
Late cues: Crying, agitated body movements, turning red

Try to feed at the early cues. A calm baby latches more easily than a crying one.

What they need (it's simpler than you think)

Warmth: Skin-to-skin, a hat, and layers. Newborns can't regulate their own temperature yet.
Food: Frequent feeding — breast or bottle. Newborn tummies are tiny (marble-sized on day one).
Closeness: Being held, hearing your heartbeat, smelling you. You are their whole world.
Sleep: They'll sleep 16–17 hours a day, but in short bursts (2–3 hours). This is normal.
Clean nappies: Expect 6–8 wet nappies a day once your milk comes in. Dark meconium poo in the first few days, then it transitions to yellow.

Normal things that might alarm you

•  Sneezing (clearing out their nasal passages, not a cold)
•  Hiccups (very common and not uncomfortable for them)
•  Startle reflex (arms fling out suddenly — completely normal)
•  Cluster feeding (wanting to feed constantly, especially in evenings)
•  Weight loss in the first few days (up to 10% is normal)
•  Noisy breathing and grunting during sleep

Something to think about

What are you most looking forward to about meeting your baby?
Hold onto that feeling. It's going to carry you through the tough moments.
