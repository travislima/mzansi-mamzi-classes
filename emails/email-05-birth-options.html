Welcome to Week 2. This week we're talking about the big one: birth.

Before we get into the stages and the details, I want to start here: you have options. Birth isn't one-size-fits-all, and knowing what's available to you helps you feel more in control — even when things don't go exactly to plan.

Let's walk through the main birth options available in South Africa.

Hospital birth

The most common choice in SA. You'll be in a hospital with doctors and nurses on hand. You can choose between a government hospital or a private hospital (with medical aid or out of pocket). Having a doula with you can make a hospital birth feel much more personal and supported.

Natural / unmedicated birth

This means giving birth without pain medication like an epidural. It can happen in a hospital, a birth centre, or at home. It's not about being brave — it's about trusting your body's ability to do what it was designed to do. Breathing techniques, movement, water, and support from your birth team all play a huge role.

Active birth

Active birth means staying upright, moving, and changing positions throughout labour rather than lying on your back. Walking, swaying, bouncing on a birth ball, getting on all fours — these positions use gravity and help your baby move down. Research shows active birth can shorten labour and reduce the need for intervention.

Water birth

Labouring in warm water is one of the best natural pain relief options. The warmth relaxes your muscles, supports your weight, and can help you feel calmer and more in control. Some moms use the pool during labour and get out to deliver; others stay in and birth in the water. Both are safe with proper support.

Home birth

Home birth is a safe option for low-risk pregnancies when attended by a registered midwife. You're in your own space, on your own terms. Your midwife brings all the necessary equipment and monitors you and baby throughout. Having had a home birth myself, I can tell you — there's something incredibly powerful about birthing in the place where you feel safest.

Caesarean section

Sometimes a C-section is planned (elective) and sometimes it becomes necessary during labour (emergency). Either way, it's still birth, and it's still valid. If this is your path, you can still ask for skin-to-skin contact immediately after, delayed cord clamping, and a gentle approach. Knowing what to expect from the procedure helps you feel prepared rather than frightened.

The most important thing

There is no "right" way to give birth. The best birth is one where you feel informed, supported, and respected. Whatever path your birth takes, you are still a warrior.
