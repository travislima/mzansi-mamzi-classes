If there's one topic that causes the most anxiety for new moms, it's feeding. So let me be honest with you upfront: breastfeeding is natural, but it's not always easy at first. And that's okay.

Most breastfeeding challenges can be solved with the right support. And if breastfeeding doesn't work out for you, your baby will still thrive. A fed baby is what matters most.

Getting started with breastfeeding

First feed: Try within the first hour after birth during skin-to-skin. Baby's instincts are strongest here. Don't stress if it's not perfect — you're both learning.
Colostrum: The thick, golden "first milk" is produced in tiny amounts but is incredibly nutrient-dense and packed with antibodies. A few drops is enough — baby's tummy is only marble-sized.
Milk coming in: Your full milk usually comes in around day 2–5. You'll know because your breasts will feel fuller and heavier. Feed frequently to establish supply.

Getting a good latch

A good latch is the single most important thing for comfortable, effective breastfeeding:

•  Baby's mouth should be wide open — wait for a big yawn-like opening
•  Aim the nipple towards the roof of their mouth
•  More areola should be visible above baby's top lip than below
•  Baby's chin should be pressed into the breast, nose free
•  You should hear swallowing, not clicking
•  It shouldn't hurt. Discomfort in the first few seconds is normal, ongoing pain is not

Common early challenges

Sore nipples: Usually caused by a shallow latch. Break the seal gently with your finger and re-latch. Lanolin cream or expressed breast milk on the nipple helps healing.
Engorgement: When your milk comes in, your breasts may feel very full and hard. Feed frequently, use warm compresses before feeding, and cold compresses after.
Cluster feeding: Baby wanting to feed constantly, especially in the evenings. This is normal and helps build your supply. It doesn't mean you don't have enough milk.
"Am I making enough?" If baby has 6+ wet nappies a day and is gaining weight, you're making enough. Trust your body.

Soothing a fussy baby

Babies cry. It's their only way of communicating a need. When you've checked the obvious (hungry, wet nappy, too hot/cold), try:

•  Skin-to-skin contact
•  Gentle swaying or rocking
•  White noise (a shushing sound, a fan, a hairdryer from a distance)
•  Swaddling (snug wrapping — many babies love the security)
•  A warm bath together
•  Going outside — fresh air and a change of scenery can reset everyone

And if nothing works and you're feeling overwhelmed, put baby down safely in their cot, walk away for a minute, and breathe. It's okay to take a break.

Get help early

If breastfeeding is painful, stressful, or not going well — ask for help early. A lactation consultant or experienced midwife can usually solve the problem in one session. Don't suffer in silence. I'm also available via our WhatsApp group — reach out any time.
