One of the biggest fears about birth is not knowing what's happening. So today, let's take away that mystery. Here's what labour actually looks like, stage by stage.

Every birth is different, but the basic stages are the same. Knowing them helps you recognise where you are and what's coming next.

How do I know labour is starting?

•  Regular contractions that get closer together, longer, and stronger
•  A "show" — a mucus plug that may be tinged with blood
•  Your waters breaking (a gush or a slow trickle)
•  Lower back pain that comes and goes
•  A feeling of pressure in your pelvis

Stage 1: Early labour (latent phase)

Cervix dilates from 0 to about 4 cm

This is usually the longest stage but the gentlest. Contractions are irregular, mild to moderate, and often feel like period cramps. You can usually stay at home during this phase.

What to do: Rest, eat light meals, drink water, take a warm bath, watch a movie. Save your energy.

Stage 1: Active labour

Cervix dilates from 4 to about 7 cm

Things pick up. Contractions become regular (every 3–5 minutes), stronger, and longer. This is usually when you head to the hospital or call your midwife if you're birthing at home. You'll need to focus and breathe through contractions.

What to do: Move, sway, use your breathing techniques. Lean on your support person. This is where your preparation pays off.

Transition

Cervix dilates from 7 to 10 cm (fully dilated)

The most intense part, but also the shortest. Contractions are very close together, very strong. You might feel shaky, nauseous, or say "I can't do this." This is actually a good sign — it means you're almost there. Almost every mom says this right before the end.

What to do: One contraction at a time. You don't have to be brave. You just have to keep going. Your body knows what to do.

Stage 2: Pushing & birth

From fully dilated until baby is born

You'll feel an overwhelming urge to push — like your body takes over. For first-time moms, this stage can take 1–3 hours. With subsequent babies, it's often much faster. Many moms describe this stage as a relief because you're finally doing something active.

What to do: Follow your body's cues. Push when you feel the urge. Listen to your midwife. You're about to meet your baby.

Stage 3: The placenta

Delivering the placenta, usually 5–30 minutes after baby

Most moms barely notice this stage because they're busy holding their baby. You'll have a few more mild contractions and the placenta will be delivered. Your midwife will check that it's complete. Meanwhile, you'll be having the most incredible skin-to-skin moment of your life.
