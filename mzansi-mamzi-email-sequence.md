## Email 2 — Content (Day 2)

**Subject:** The emotional rollercoaster nobody talks about

Hi mama,

Can we talk about something that doesn't get nearly enough airtime? The emotional side of pregnancy.

Everyone asks how you're feeling physically. But nobody really asks about the rest of it. The excitement that suddenly turns into anxiety at 2am. The guilt about not feeling excited enough. The irritability that comes out of nowhere. The crying at adverts.

Here's the thing: all of it is normal.

**Your hormones are doing incredible things right now.** Progesterone and oestrogen are at levels your body has never experienced before. Your brain is literally rewiring itself to prepare for motherhood. Of course your emotions are all over the place.

**What might be showing up for you:**

- Mood swings that feel extreme or unpredictable
- Anxiety about the birth, the baby, or whether you'll be a good mom
- Feeling overwhelmed by decisions (the nursery, the birth plan, the name)
- Irritability with your partner, family, or colleagues
- Unexpected sadness or tearfulness
- Excitement followed immediately by fear

**What actually helps:**

- **Name it.** "I'm feeling anxious right now" is more powerful than you think. Once you name the feeling, it loses some of its grip.
- **Lower the bar.** You don't need to have everything figured out. You don't need to feel magical every day. Some days you just need to get through the day, and that's enough.
- **Move your body.** Even a 15-minute walk can shift your mood. Gentle movement helps regulate those hormone surges.
- **Talk about it.** Tell your partner, a friend, or your midwife. Saying "I'm struggling a bit" is not weakness. It's wisdom.
- **Limit the noise.** If social media is making you feel worse, step away. If a particular person's advice is stressing you out, it's okay to set a boundary.

**When to reach out for more support:**
If you're feeling persistently low, anxious to the point where it's affecting your daily life, or having thoughts that frighten you, please talk to your midwife or doctor. Antenatal depression and anxiety are real, they're common, and they're treatable. There is no shame in asking for help.

## Email 3 — Content (Day 4)

**Subject:** Your body right now (and what to do about the niggles)

Hi mama,

Let's talk about the stuff that's probably bugging you right now. The niggles, the aches, the things that nobody mentioned in the happy pregnancy announcements.

Your body is doing something extraordinary. It's building a human. But that process comes with some side effects that range from mildly annoying to genuinely uncomfortable. The good news? Most of them are manageable, and most of them are temporary.

**Nausea and morning sickness**
(Which should really be called "all day sickness" because it doesn't care what time it is.)

- Eat small amounts often, even if you don't feel like it. An empty stomach makes it worse.
- Ginger really does help. Ginger tea, ginger biscuits, even ginger sweets.
- Keep plain crackers next to your bed and eat a few before you get up.
- Avoid strong smells where you can.
- If you can't keep anything down, talk to your midwife or doctor. There are safe medications that can help.

**Constipation**
Very common, thanks to progesterone slowing down your digestive system.

- Water, water, water. More than you think you need.
- Fibre-rich foods: fruit, vegetables, whole grains.
- Gentle movement helps get things moving (literally).
- Prune juice is not glamorous, but it works.

**Backache and pelvic pain**
Your ligaments are softening (thanks, relaxin hormone) and your centre of gravity is shifting.

- Avoid standing for long periods.
- Sleep with a pillow between your knees.
- Gentle stretching and prenatal yoga can make a big difference.
- A warm (not hot) bath can ease aching muscles.

**Leg cramps**
Often worse at night. Lovely timing.

- Stay hydrated.
- Stretch your calves before bed.
- Magnesium-rich foods (bananas, nuts, dark leafy greens) may help.
- When a cramp hits, flex your foot (toes towards your shin) rather than pointing it.

**Swelling**
Some swelling in your feet and ankles is normal, especially later in pregnancy.

- Put your feet up when you can.
- Avoid standing or sitting in one position for too long.
- Stay hydrated (yes, drinking more water actually helps with swelling).
- If swelling is sudden, severe, or in your face and hands, contact your midwife or doctor immediately as this could be a sign of something that needs attention.

**Things to avoid:**
- Alcohol
- Smoking
- Raw or undercooked meat, eggs, and fish
- Unpasteurised dairy
- High-mercury fish (swordfish, king mackerel)
- Excessive caffeine (1-2 cups of coffee a day is generally fine)
- Cat litter (toxoplasmosis risk)
- Very hot baths or saunas

**Warning signs to watch for:**
Contact your midwife or doctor if you experience:
- Vaginal bleeding
- Severe headache that won't go away
- Vision changes (blurring, seeing spots)
- Sudden severe swelling of face or hands
- Decreased baby movements (after 28 weeks)
- Fever
- Painful urination
- Fluid leaking from the vagina

## Email 5 — Content (Day 7)

**Subject:** Birth is not what you think it is

Hi mama,

Before we dive into the practical stuff this week (stages of labour, pain relief, all of that), I want to start here: birth is probably not what you think it is.

If your main source of information about birth has been movies, TV shows, or dramatic stories from well-meaning friends and family, you've been given a very skewed picture. Birth on screen is always sudden, dramatic, and terrifying. The water breaks in a restaurant, there's screaming, someone's speeding to the hospital, and a baby appears in about 3 minutes.

Real birth? It's usually slower. Quieter. More powerful. And you're more capable of it than you've been led to believe.

**Some things that might surprise you:**

- **Labour usually starts gently.** Early contractions often feel like period cramps or a backache. Most women spend the early part of labour at home, carrying on with normal life.
- **It takes time.** First-time labour averages 12-18 hours (sometimes more, sometimes less). This isn't 12 hours of intense pain. Much of it is manageable, and the intense part is usually the shortest.
- **Your body knows what to do.** You don't need to "learn" how to give birth. Your body already knows. What you need is to feel safe, supported, and informed enough to trust the process.
- **You have choices.** Where you give birth, who's in the room, what positions you use, what pain relief you want. These are your decisions to make.
- **Birth partners matter.** Having someone calm and supportive with you makes a measurable difference. This is why partners are welcome at our Friday sessions.

**The different ways birth can happen:**

- **Natural/vaginal birth:** The most common. Your body labours, your cervix dilates, and you push your baby out. This can happen at hospital, a birth centre, or at home.
- **Active birth:** Staying mobile and upright during labour, using gravity and movement to help. This isn't a separate "type" of birth, it's an approach.
- **Water birth:** Labouring or delivering in a birth pool. The warm water helps with pain and relaxation. Available at some hospitals, birth centres, and for home births.
- **Assisted delivery:** Sometimes baby needs a little help coming out, using forceps or vacuum (ventouse). This is more common with first babies.
- **Caesarean section:** Either planned (elective) or unplanned (emergency). About 1 in 4 births in South Africa are by C-section. We'll talk more about this.
- **Induction:** When labour is started artificially, usually because of medical reasons. We'll cover when and why this happens.

**The most important thing I want you to take from this email:**

Fear makes birth harder. Not because you're weak, but because fear triggers your body's stress response, which works directly against the hormones that drive labour. The more informed and calm you feel going in, the better your body can do its job.

That's what these classes are for. Not to tell you what to do, but to help you feel ready for whatever your birth looks like.

## Email 6 — Content (Day 9)

**Subject:** What happens during labour (stage by stage)

Hi mama,

Okay, let's get practical. Here's what actually happens during labour, stage by stage. Knowing what to expect takes so much of the fear away.

**First Stage: The Opening**
This is the longest stage. Your cervix needs to dilate (open) from 0 to 10 centimetres. It happens in two phases:

*Early labour (0-4cm):*
- Contractions are mild to moderate, often 15-30 minutes apart, lasting 30-45 seconds.
- This can last hours (or even a day or two with first babies). Don't rush to the hospital.
- What to do: carry on with life. Eat, drink, rest, walk, watch a movie. Time your contractions but don't obsess.
- This is usually manageable. Think period cramps.

*Active labour (4-7cm):*
- Contractions get stronger, closer together (3-5 minutes apart), lasting about 60 seconds.
- This is when things get real. You'll need to focus through contractions.
- This is usually when you'd go to the hospital or call your midwife.
- What helps: movement, breathing, warm water, massage, changing positions.

**Transition (7-10cm):**
- The most intense part. Contractions are very strong, 2-3 minutes apart, lasting 60-90 seconds.
- This is the part that's hardest, but it's also the shortest (usually 30 minutes to 2 hours).
- You might feel shaky, nauseous, overwhelmed, or like you can't do this. This is completely normal and actually a sign that you're close.
- What helps: one contraction at a time. Literally just get through this one. Then the next one. Your support person is crucial here.

**Second Stage: The Pushing**
- Your cervix is fully dilated. You'll feel an overwhelming urge to push (like needing to have a bowel movement).
- This can last from a few minutes to a couple of hours (usually longer with first babies).
- You push with your contractions and rest between them.
- Positions that help: squatting, hands and knees, side-lying, supported squat. Lying flat on your back is actually one of the worst positions (despite what you see on TV) because it works against gravity.
- And then... your baby is born.

**Third Stage: The Placenta**
- After baby is born, you still need to deliver the placenta. This usually happens within 5-30 minutes.
- You might barely notice this because you'll be focused on your baby.
- Your midwife or doctor will check that the placenta is complete.

**Positions for labour:**

Here's something important: you don't have to lie in bed during labour. In fact, being upright and mobile helps labour progress and can reduce pain.

- **Walking and swaying** during early labour
- **Leaning forward** over a ball, bed, or your partner
- **Hands and knees** (great for back pain)
- **Squatting** (opens the pelvis)
- **Side-lying** (good for resting between contractions)
- **Rocking on a birth ball**

Your body will often tell you what position feels right. Listen to it.

## Email 7 — Content (Day 11)

**Subject:** Pain relief, interventions, and knowing your options

Hi mama,

Let's talk about pain management and medical interventions. This isn't about judging any choices. It's about making sure you know what's available so you can make informed decisions when the time comes.

**Non-medical pain relief:**

These are things you can do yourself or with your support person:

- **Breathing techniques.** Slow, deep breathing during contractions. In through the nose, out through the mouth. This isn't just "nice to do," it actually helps your body release endorphins (your natural painkillers).
- **Warm water.** A bath or shower during labour. The relief can be dramatic. Many women describe getting into warm water as going from a 7 to a 4 on the pain scale.
- **Movement and position changes.** Staying mobile and upright. Rocking, swaying, walking.
- **Massage and counter-pressure.** Your birth partner pressing firmly on your lower back during contractions. Tennis balls work well for this.
- **TENS machine.** A small device that sends electrical impulses through pads on your back. Works best in early labour. You can hire these.
- **Visualisation and focus.** Having a focal point, listening to music or a meditation, visualising your cervix opening.
- **Heat packs.** On your lower back or abdomen.

**Medical pain relief:**

- **Entonox (gas and air).** You breathe it through a mouthpiece during contractions. It takes the edge off without removing sensation completely. You control it yourself. Wears off quickly. Available at most hospitals.
- **Pethidine (injection).** An opioid painkiller given by injection. Takes about 20 minutes to work, lasts 2-4 hours. Can make you drowsy. Can't be given too close to delivery as it can affect baby's breathing. Baby might be sleepy.
- **Epidural.** An anaesthetic injected into the space around your spinal cord. Gives significant to complete pain relief from the waist down. You'll need a drip, continuous monitoring, and a catheter. You can't walk around. It's very effective for pain but does change the experience of labour. Takes about 20 minutes to place and 15 minutes to work. Can be topped up.

**Interventions you should know about:**

- **Induction.** Starting labour artificially. Done when it's medically safer for baby to be born than to stay inside (overdue, pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, waters broken without labour starting, etc.). Methods include prostaglandin gel or pessary, artificial rupture of membranes, or an oxytocin drip.
- **Continuous monitoring.** Straps on your belly that continuously track baby's heart rate and your contractions. Limits your movement but gives detailed information. Usually required with an epidural or induction.
- **Episiotomy.** A small cut to widen the vaginal opening during delivery. Done if baby needs to come out quickly or to prevent a severe tear. Usually done with local anaesthetic. Stitched up afterwards.
- **Assisted delivery (forceps or vacuum).** Used when baby needs help coming out, usually because of exhaustion, baby's position, or baby's heart rate. Done by a doctor.
- **Caesarean section.** Major surgery to deliver baby through an incision in your abdomen. Planned C-sections are scheduled in advance (for reasons like baby's position, previous C-section, medical conditions). Emergency C-sections happen during labour when vaginal delivery isn't safe. Recovery takes longer than vaginal birth (usually 6-8 weeks).

**How to think about all of this:**

Have preferences, but hold them loosely. A birth plan is useful because it makes you think through your options in advance. But birth is unpredictable, and the goal is a healthy mom and a healthy baby, however that happens.

Know what's available. Ask questions. The right question is always: "Is my baby and I safe? What are our options? What do you recommend and why?"

You have the right to informed consent. Nobody should do anything to you without explaining what, why, and getting your agreement (except in a true emergency).

## Email 9 — Content (Day 14)

**Subject:** 5 things about your newborn that will surprise you

Hi mama,

Welcome to Week 3. We're talking about your baby and life after birth. Let's start with some things about newborns that catch most new parents off guard.

**1. They don't look like the babies in the adverts.**
Newborns are often blotchy, wrinkly, and a bit swollen. They might have a cone-shaped head (from the birth canal), white waxy stuff on their skin (vernix, it's protective and good for them), fine body hair (lanugo, it falls off), and puffy eyes. They're beautiful, but they're also a bit... alien-looking. This is normal. They pink up and smooth out within days.

**2. They sleep a lot, but never when you want them to.**
Newborns sleep 16-17 hours a day, but in short bursts of 2-4 hours. They don't know the difference between day and night yet. This is the hardest part of the early days for most parents. It gets better, but the first few weeks are a survival game. Sleep when baby sleeps is cliched but genuinely good advice.

**3. They cry, and sometimes you won't know why.**
Babies cry because they're hungry, tired, uncomfortable, overstimulated, or sometimes just because. If you've fed them, changed them, burped them, and cuddled them and they're still crying, you haven't failed. Some babies just need to cry for a bit. Put them down safely, take a breath, and try again. It's not a reflection of your parenting.

**4. Their poo will go through a journey.**
First poo (meconium) is black and sticky like tar. Then it changes to greenish, then to yellow and seedy (if breastfed) or tan and firmer (if formula-fed). Breastfed babies can poo after every feed or go several days without one. Both are normal. You will discuss poo more than you ever imagined possible.

**5. They can't be spoiled.**
You cannot spoil a newborn. Holding them, responding to their cries, feeding on demand, having skin-to-skin contact: this isn't spoiling, it's exactly what they need. Newborns need to feel safe and close to you. Follow your instincts on this one, not outdated advice about "making a rod for your back."

**What your baby actually needs in the first days:**

- Warmth (skin-to-skin is the best way)
- Feeding (on demand, whether breast or bottle)
- Clean nappy
- To feel safe and held
- That's pretty much it

## Email 10 — Content (Day 16)

**Subject:** Breastfeeding, feeding, and soothing your baby

Hi mama,

Breastfeeding. It's natural, but that doesn't mean it's easy. Let's be honest about what to expect and how to give yourself the best start.

**The first feed:**
Ideally, baby goes straight onto your chest after birth (skin-to-skin) and has their first feed within the first hour. Babies are often alert and instinctively root for the breast in this first hour. This first feed is important for establishing breastfeeding and for bonding.

**Colostrum:**
For the first 2-3 days, you produce colostrum, not milk. It's thick, yellowish, and comes in tiny amounts. This is normal. Colostrum is incredibly rich in antibodies and is exactly what your baby needs. A newborn's stomach is the size of a marble on day 1. They don't need much.

**When your milk "comes in":**
Around day 3-5, your milk transitions from colostrum to mature milk. Your breasts may feel full, hard, and tender. This is called engorgement and it settles within a day or two. Feed frequently to relieve it. Warm compresses before feeding and cold compresses after can help.

**Getting the latch right:**
This is the most important thing. A good latch means:
- Baby's mouth is wide open
- They take in a big mouthful of breast (not just the nipple)
- Their chin is touching the breast
- You can see more areola above the top lip than below the bottom lip
- It feels like a strong pull, but not painful

If it hurts, gently break the suction (slip your finger into the corner of baby's mouth) and try again. A bad latch is the number one cause of sore nipples and feeding problems.

**How often to feed:**
Newborns feed 8-12 times in 24 hours. Yes, that's a lot. Especially in the first few weeks, feeding feels like all you do. This is normal and it's how your body learns how much milk to make. Feed on demand (when baby shows hunger cues) rather than on a schedule.

**Hunger cues to watch for:**
- Rooting (turning head, opening mouth)
- Sucking on hands or fingers
- Lip smacking
- Fussing
- Crying is a late hunger cue. Try to catch the earlier signs.

**If breastfeeding doesn't work out:**
Some moms can't breastfeed, some choose not to, and some do a combination of breast and formula. A fed baby is the goal. If you're struggling, ask for help early (from your midwife, a lactation consultant, or La Leche League). But also know that formula is a perfectly good way to feed your baby. Don't let anyone make you feel guilty about how you feed your child.

**Soothing your baby:**

When baby is fussy and it's not hunger or a nappy change, try:
- **Skin-to-skin.** Works almost magically in the early weeks.
- **Swaddling.** Wrapping baby snugly (but not too tightly, and never over the face). Many babies find this calming.
- **Movement.** Gentle rocking, swaying, walking. A car ride or pram walk often works too.
- **White noise.** A shushing sound, a fan, a white noise app. Babies are used to constant noise from the womb.
- **Sucking.** A dummy/pacifier (if you're comfortable with it) or letting baby suck on your clean finger.

**Colic:**
Some babies cry for extended periods (3+ hours) for no apparent reason, often in the evening. This is called colic. It's not caused by anything you're doing wrong. It usually starts around 2-3 weeks and peaks around 6 weeks. It's exhausting and distressing. If you're dealing with this, you are not failing. Ask for help, take breaks, and know that it passes.

## Email 11 — Content (Day 18)

**Subject:** Your body after birth (and how to take care of yourself)

Hi mama,

We spend so much time focusing on the baby that we forget: you also need to recover. Your body just did something monumental, and it needs care. Let's talk about what happens after.

**Vaginal bleeding (lochia):**
You will bleed after birth, whether you had a vaginal delivery or C-section. This is your uterus shedding its lining. It's like a heavy period at first, then tapers off over 4-6 weeks. Use maternity pads (not tampons). It starts red, becomes pinkish-brown, then yellowish-white. If bleeding suddenly gets heavier, you pass large clots, or it smells bad, contact your midwife or doctor.

**Afterpains:**
Your uterus contracts back to its pre-pregnancy size. These cramps can be quite sore, especially while breastfeeding (breastfeeding triggers oxytocin, which causes contractions). They're usually worse with second and subsequent babies. Paracetamol and ibuprofen help. A heat pack on your tummy helps too.

**Perineum care (vaginal delivery):**
Whether you tore or had an episiotomy, the area will be sore. To help healing:
- Pour warm water over the area when you wee (it stings less).
- Pat dry gently (don't rub).
- Sit on a soft pillow or folded towel.
- Ice packs in a cloth can help with swelling in the first 24-48 hours.
- Keep the area clean and change pads regularly.
- Stitches dissolve on their own. If pain increases rather than decreases, or there's a bad smell, see your midwife.

**C-section recovery:**
If you have a C-section, remember this is major abdominal surgery.
- You'll stay in hospital for 2-4 days.
- Moving and walking (gently) as soon as possible helps recovery.
- Don't lift anything heavier than your baby for 6 weeks.
- Hold a pillow against your incision when coughing, sneezing, or laughing.
- Keep the wound clean and dry.
- Pain medication is important. Take it as prescribed. Don't try to be brave.
- Full recovery takes 6-8 weeks (sometimes longer). Be patient with yourself.

**Your emotions after birth:**
The "baby blues" are very common, affecting up to 80% of new moms. Around day 3-5 (when your milk comes in and your hormones crash), you may feel tearful, overwhelmed, anxious, or irritable. This usually passes within a week or two.

Postnatal depression is different. It's more persistent, more intense, and can start any time in the first year. Signs include:
- Persistent sadness or emptiness
- Feeling disconnected from your baby
- Difficulty sleeping (even when baby sleeps)
- Loss of interest in things you normally enjoy
- Feeling like you're not a good mom
- Scary or intrusive thoughts

If this sounds like you, please reach out. To your midwife, your doctor, your partner, a friend. Postnatal depression is not your fault, and it's treatable. You deserve help.

**Practical survival tips for the first weeks:**

- **Accept help.** When someone offers to cook, clean, hold the baby while you shower, say yes. This is not weakness.
- **Lower your standards.** The house will be messy. You will live in pyjamas. This is temporary and completely fine.
- **Eat and drink.** You need fuel, especially if you're breastfeeding. Stock the freezer before baby comes. Keep water and snacks wherever you feed.
- **Limit visitors.** You don't owe anyone access to your baby. It's okay to say "not today."
- **Get outside.** Even a short walk around the block. Fresh air and sunlight help everything.
- **Be kind to yourself.** You're learning something completely new. Give yourself the same grace you'd give a friend.
